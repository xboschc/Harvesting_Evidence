GRADE Evidence Profiles

How to Interpret GRADE Evidence Profiles

Limitations: lack of allocation concealment, lack of blinding, incomplete accounting of patients and outcome events, selective outcome reporting, failure to develop and apply appropriate eligibility criteria (inclusion of control population), flawed measurement of both exposure and outcome, failure to adequately control confounding, incomplete or inadequately short follow-up, among others.

 

Inconsistency: refers to an unexplained heterogeneity of results. Widely differing estimates of the treatment effect (i.e. heterogeneity or variability in results) across studies suggest true differences in underlying treatment effect. When heterogeneity exists, but investigators fail to identify a plausible explanation, the quality of evidence should be downgraded by one or two levels, depending on the magnitude of the inconsistency in the results.

 

Indirectness: if the evidence answers directly the health care question asked and if any indirectness of available evidence may have been serious enough to downgrade the quality of evidence for this outcome.

 

Imprecision: if the results are precise enough and if any imprecision of the results may have been serious enough to downgrade the quality of evidence for this outcome.

 

Quality of Evidence

[++++] High quality: further research is very unlikely to change our confidence in the estimate of effect.
[+++~] Moderate quality: further research is likely to have an important impact on our confidence in the estimate of effect and may change the estimate.
[++~~] Low quality: further research is very likely to have an important impact on our confidence in the estimate of effect and is likely to change the estimate.
[+~ ~ ~] Very low quality: we are very uncertain about the estimate

 

Importance: critical, important but not critical, of limited importance.
