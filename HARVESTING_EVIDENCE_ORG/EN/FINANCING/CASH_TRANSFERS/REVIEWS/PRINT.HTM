Available evidence from reviews

Lagarde 2007 conducted a systematic review to assess the effectiveness of conditional monetary transfers on access to and use of health services, and on health outcomes in LMIC.

 

Criteria for inclusion in a study were: a randomized controlled trial, interrupted time series analysis, or a controlled before and after study, and additionally a measure of health care utilization, health expenditure, or health outcome. Six studies met these criteria.

 

Most of the six studies involved large-scale conditional cash transfer programs in Latin America. In general, these programs provided cash payments to targeted low-income households if they utilized preventive health care services. In some cases, payments were conditional on school attendance by children. One study, in Africa, involved provision of financial incentives for collection of HIV test results.

 

The main outcomes measured by the studies were health care utilization or access to health care, household health expenditure, or health or nutritional outcomes.

 

Owing to methodology limitations of the studies, statistical pooling of results was not attempted by the authors of the review.

 

Main findings:

  • heatlh related behaviours, use of services, improvement of nutritional status: the review identifies a fairly consistent picture of the effects of conditional cash transfers.
  • Health status: their overall impact is less clear.

 

Highlighted by authors:

  • Five studies reported that conditional cash transfers increased use of health services.
  • Four studies that measured the impact of conditional cash transfer programs on immunization coverage showed unclear effects.
  • Impact on nutritional outcomes, which was assessed in four studies, were generally positive, but limited to certain subgroups of beneficiaries.
  • Other health outcomes were reported for three conditional cash transfer programs. Effects on objectively measured health outcomes were mixed but there were positive effects on mothers’ reports of their children’s health.
  • Only one study tried to measure the relative effects of cash transfers of differing values.

 

Considerations:

  • The pathways by which the effects of conditional cash transfers are achieved have not been well-studied and are not well understood.
  • The conditional cash transfer programs studied involved significant flows of money, but the cost-effectiveness of conditional cash transfer programs compared with classic supply-side interventions has not been assessed.
  • Both health status and nutritional outcomes may be influenced by different components of the programs that were studied, eg by nutritional supplements, by better household diet (due to increased disposable income), and by improved knowledge resulting from mothers attending health education sessions. The included studies were not able to determine the relative importance of different components of the programs.

    Additional notes:

    Applicability:

     

    • The review included a small number of studies, nearly all of which were conducted in Latin America. Generalisability to other regions cannot be assumed.
    • In LIC, where access to effective interventions is very limited, supply-side obstacles (e.g. geographical inaccessibility or poor quality of services) may need to be tackled before introducing conditional cash transfer programs.
    • Means testing is likely to be expensive or impossible in settings where reliable information systems are not yet the norm. If, for this reason, eligibility is expanded to the entire population, cost-effectiveness will be especially critical to sustainability.
    • Efficient and secure systems for making conditional cash payments to households are also vital to the success of such programs.
    • In the design and implementation of conditional cash transfer programmes the risk of creating perverse incentives (i.e. unintended undesirable effects) must be managed and monitored.

     

     

    Lagarde 2007 has been summarised by SUPPORT (August 2008).

     

     

    The Lagarde 2007 review appeared in an issue of the The Journal of the American Medical Association (JAMA)  dedicated to 'Poverty and Human Development'.Some other articles included: "Informing Resource-Poor Populations and the Delivery of Entitled Health and Social Services in Rural India: A Cluster Randomized Controlled Trial", "Improving Child Survival Through Environmental and Nutritional Interventions: The Importance of Targeting Interventions Toward the Poor" and "Clinical Outcomes and CD4 Cell Response in Children Receiving Antiretroviral Therapy at Primary Health Care Facilities in Zambia".
