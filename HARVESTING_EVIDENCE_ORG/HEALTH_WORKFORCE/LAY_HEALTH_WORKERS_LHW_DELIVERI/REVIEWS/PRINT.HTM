Avilable Evidence from Reviews

Lewin 2005 examined whether healthcare or promotion activities carried out by LHW would improve the delivery of services or the health status, in primary healthcare settings.

 

Forty three studies were included in this review, all of them (simple or cluster) randomised controlled trials. 12 of them are specially relevant to this site, dealing with maternal and child health: immunisation (three studies), breast-feeding promotion (five studies) and breast cancer (four studies).

 

The methodological quality of the studies was assessed using a previously published set of criteria

 

Findings:

 

  • Immunisation outcomes

Considering the three studies addressing immunisation uptake as the final outcome, LHW were effective: participants receiving LHW support were more likely (1.30 times, 95% confidence interval 1.14 to 1.48) to be adequately immunised. The results taking into account individual studies have been sumarrised in the table below. The effects in the case of children are smaller.

The relative risk expresses how a participant in the intervention group (LHW) is more likely to receive appropriate immunisation than a participant in the control group. If the RR is greater than 1, then the intervention is considered as effective. In parenthesis, 95% confidence intervals (if the range in the confidence interval includes the '1', then the RR should be interpreted as uncertain).

 

  • Breastfeeding outcomes

Again, LHW increased the probability of initiating and continuing breast-feeding (adjusted RR of 1.69 (0.914 to 3.12) and 2.93 (0.88 to 9.71)). However, included studies showed a wide range of different results and these summary RR (pooled) should be interpreted with caution.

 

  • Promotion of breast cancer screening outcome

There was little evidence on the effects of LHW in promoting breast cancer screening (RR 1.05, 0.99 to 1.12).

 

Considerations:

  • In experimental situations, such as those reported in studies evaluation LHW, the intensity of the interventions (e.g. training and supervision of LHW) tends to be much higher than in real life situations. Therefore, LHW may work in a given setting, but in another one results may not be as expected.
  • Sometimes LHW have been used as an 'extension' of the usual care, but sometimes as 'substitutes' of professional care. The possibility of doing harm by substituting professional care by LHW has to be taken seriously.
  • No conclusions on costs could be drawn. Depending on the combined interventions, the way of implementation and the intensity of training and supervision, costs could eventually rise considerably.
