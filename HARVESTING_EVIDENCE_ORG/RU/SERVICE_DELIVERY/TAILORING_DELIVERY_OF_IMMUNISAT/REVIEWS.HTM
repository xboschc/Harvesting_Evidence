Avilable Evidence from Reviews

Two complementary reviews examined interventions related to the way immunisation is delivered and its effects on immunisation coverage, one based on grey literature (Batt 2004) and the other based on published articles (Pegurri 2005).

 

The inclusion criteria of studies in both reviews were the same. Any study design was included which would allow comparison of coverage, explicitly, in the time between two coverage values.

 

The methodological quality of the studies was assessed in both reviews using the same set of criteria previously published in the literature. Some studies were excluded based on this assessment, leaving 24 'interventions' in 34 studies in Batt 2004 and 29 'interventions' in 40 studies in Pegurri 2005.

 

The studies included in both reviews examined different interventions including monitoring and supervision, changes in model of provider or payer, increasing awareness, shortening waiting time and missed opportunities, mass campaigns, re-organisation of the health system, lay health workers, outreach teams, channelling, and reminders. Although some of the interventions could have been included under other building blocks, most of them (and the number of studies addressing them) related to the first building block - the way services are delivered.

 

Main findings (coverage of fully vaccinated children or DTP3 if full coverage was unavailable):

  • Baseline immunisation coverage in included studies combined:
    • 44% (SD 21; range 17 to 84), Batt 2004
    • 34% (SD 19; range 3 to 65), Pegurri 2005
  • Average change in coverage:
    • 20% (SD 9, range -8 to 55), Batt 2004
    • 27% (SD 19, range -1 to 72), Pegurri 2005

 

 Highlighted by the authors of the review:

  • Introduction of an Integrated Supervisory Checklist (Philippines): change in coverage by -0.2% (Batt 2004).
  • Introduction of National Plan of Action (Guatemala): change in coverage by -7.5% (Batt 2004).
  • Mass campaign (Senegal) had negative effects on coverage when measured at mid term (Pegurri 2005).
  • Interventions with highest impact on immunisation coverage:
    • health education campaigns (Batt 2004)
    • changes in provider and payer model (Batt 2004) 
    • mass campaigns (Batt 2004)
    • lay health workers (Pegurri 2005)
    • channelling (Pegurri 2005)

 

Cost per dose delivered (fully vaccinated child):

  • In mass campaings costs were 4 to 9 times higher than in routine immunisation
  • (Batt 2004) and ranged from USD 7 to 222 per DPT3 dose and fully vaccinated child (Pegurri 2005).

 

Considerations:

  • The description of the context was partial in 43% of the studies and inadequate in 50% of the studies (Pegurri 2005).
  • Some interventions were implemented in combination (e.g. community education and in-service training) and they are hardly described to allow replication (acknowledged by the authors of the reviews).
  • Reviews did not report on the SD or range of the size of the effects of each individual intervention.
  • Pegurri 2005 checked the correlation between baseline coverage levels and the size of the observed effects, finding no relation.
  • Effects on coverage were usually measured shortly after the intervention took place.
