Available Evidence from Reviews

Two reviews, Bosch-Capblanch 2008 and Ross-Degnan 1997, included 'administrative' or 'managerial interventions':

  • to improve health workers performance through supervision (Bosch-Capblanch 2008)
  • to improve prescription practices (Ross-Degnan 1997)

 

Bosch-Capblanch 2008 aimed at scoping the concept of supervision and provide evidence on its implementation and its effects. All study designs were included, but experimental and quasi-experimental studies were confined to assess the evidence on the effects of supervision. The methodological quality of experimental or quasi-experimental studies was assessed using a published set of criteria. However, no study was excluded based on the quality assessment.

 

In the case of Ross-Degnan 1997, the quality of the studies was defined according to their design: randomised controlled trials, pre-post designs with control groups and time series were considered as good quality. Other desgins were pre-post studies without control or post-only studies.

 

Interventions:

  • training or workshops of professionals (12 studies of high quality in Ross-Degnan 1997)
  • training of LHW in community case management (12 high quality studies in Ross-Degnan 1997)
  • Administrative or managerial interventions:
    • facilitation of a group process (similar to seminars) (Ross-Degnan 1997)
    • guidelines (Ross-Degnan 1997) 
    • supervision and monitoring (Bosch-Capblanch 2008, Ross-Degnan 1997)
  • drugs programmes and drug supplies (Ross-Degnan 1997)

 

Bosch-Capblanch 2008 is a scoping review on supervision structured around three objectives: (1) to summarise what guidelines and manuals say about supervision; (2) to compare these features with reports describing supervision in practice; and (3) to appraise the evidence of the effects of supervision on performance. The review focused on primary health care in LMIC.

 

 

Featuring supervision (from Bosch-Capblanch 2008):

  • The main features of supervision, from the eight guidelines or manuals were that supervision is the link between the central tier of the health systems and the periphery; it plays an essential role in motivating staff; includes problem solving, reviewing records and observation of clinical practices; and most often is carried out through in-site visits.
  • The reports on how supervision took place revealed that most of the times was 'general', involving primary health care as a whole; supervision for specific programmes included: reproductive health, child health, fever / malaria and drug management.
  • In-site visits where the most common approach although in some cases there were also meetings in the central tier of the system.
  • The frequency of supervision encounters was 6 times a year (median across studies), although in many occasions it was stated that it took place less frequently than planned.
  • The managerial tasks were the most frequently carried out (collection of health related data, management of resources and supplies).

 

Findings in relation to specific outcomes:

  •  The effects of supervision varied according to the intervention and outcome:
    • Health outcomes
      • community case management and reduction of mortality: moderate to high impacts reported (Ross-Degnan 1997)
      • LHW supervised by health units staff: reduction in malaria incidence (rate ratios 1.9, 95% CI 1.6 to 2.1) (Bosch-Capblanch 2008)
      • complex intervention including supervision of VHW and TBA: reduction in infant mortality over 15 years (rate ratios of 0.74 up to 1.14, depending on the time period considered) (Bosch-Capblanch 2008)
    • Utilisation / coverage
      • complex intervention in the context of a nutritional programme (Curtale 1995): improvement in immunisation coverage (statistically significant) (Bosch-Capblanch 2008);
    • Prescription / management
      • effects of training on prescription: three interventions showed large improvements, seven moderate and four little or no impact (Ross-Degnan 1997)
      • distribution of guidelines: no impact (Ross-Degnan 1997);
      • donation of goods or guidelines and supervision: improvement on prescription habits (with statistically significant results) (Bosch-Capblanch 2008 and Ross-Degnan 1997)
      • supervision specific tools (supervisory checklist): statistically significant improvement in patient management (Bosch-Capblanch 2008 and Ross-Degnan 1997)
      • Other indicators related to management, adherence to treatment guidelines, record keeping, were inconsistent (Bosch-Capblanch 2008)
    • costs of supervision: where costs were reported, they were considered as expensive (supervision was the single most expensive, in Grosskurth 2000; Costello 2001 reported that local governments could not afford the costs of supervisory visits) (Bosch-Capblanch 2008)

 

Highlighted by the authors of the review:

 

  • 'Supervision' (i.e. a link between the different tiers of the health system) is not an option. The link needs to be there. The issue is (1) how best implement supervision and (2) how affordable can it be.
  • Supervision has to be a tool to improve performance and therefore embedded in the responsiblity chain of supervisees, supervisors and high level managers. This demands political commitment.
  • Reviews with a broad spectrum of interventions and outcomes pose some challenges in the interpretation of results. Training, and ohter interventions, can be conducted in many differeny ways and, furthermore, pooling or summarising the effects of interventions on the selected outcomes becomes very complicated.

    Additional Notes:

    Several reviews have explored interventions to improve health workers performance and the link between supervision and performance. Rowe 2005 did an overview of reviews of determinants of health workers performance and examined a range of interventions. Rowe 2005 concluded that “management approaches”, such as supervision, audit and feedback, generally had moderate to large effects, based on the included reviews. Rowe 2005 cited other reviews that mentioned supervision, such as Ross-Degnan 1997, included here.

     

    Another review examined various interventions on drug prescribing, and explicitly looked at supervision in relation to clinical guidelines, where it was conceived as a complementary intervention to training and helped in sustaining intervention effects with treatment guidelines programmes (WHO 2001). This review (WHO 2001) included a subset of studies already approached in the review of Grimshaw 2004.
