Available Evidence from Reviews

Grilli 2002 performed a Cochrane systematic review to assess the impact of mass media interventions at the population level to promote (or discourage) the use of health care services.

 

Twenty interrupted time series studies were included, of which 15 looked at planned mass media campaigns to encourage use of certain health services, and five considered media coverage of health related issues. Most of the health issues and services studied involved non-communicable diseases such as screening for various cancers. Three studies focused on HIV testing and two focused on immunisation uptake.

 

Various studies considered interventions using print media, radio, or television, or combinations of more than one of these. All but one of the 20 studies were done in high income countries.

 

The authors of the review noted that the overall methodological quality of the studies was variable. Fifteen of the 20 studies either did not involve any statistical analysis or used inappropriate statistical tests.

 

Main findings:

  • mass media was effective in improving use of health services (in 19 of 20 studies) both following campaigns planned by health organizations, and following media-led coverage. Although there were differences in the extent of the effects, the direction of effect was consistent. This suggests that mass media can improve health services utilization, regardless of whether the intervention is planned by health professional bodies or initiated by journalists.

 

Highlighted by the authors:

  • some of the studies on mass media and uptake of HIV tests suggest a non-specific impact, i.e., an increase in the overall volume of tests performed across all risk groups.

 

 

Considerations:

  •  it was not possible to draw firm conclusions about the characteristics of successful campaigns, or to identify differences in the effect of planned campaigns and unplanned coverage
  • it is not clear whether the impact of mass media on clinical practice was specific, i.e., resulting in more appropriate use of services by patients who can most benefit from them, or non-specific, resulting in changes in overall rates of use without affecting the appropriateness of how health services are utilized
  • the effects observed in the studies may be associated with changes in the behavior of health care providers (supply) and/or the behavior of users (demand) but the studies do not allow such changes to be quantified separately
  • the studies did not reveal whether the observed effects were long-lasting or transient
  • the cost effectiveness of mass media interventions has not been examined in this review
  • the findings of this review may be affected by publication bias
