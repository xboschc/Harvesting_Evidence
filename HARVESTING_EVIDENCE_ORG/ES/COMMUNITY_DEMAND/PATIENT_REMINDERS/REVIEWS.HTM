Available Evidence from Reviews

One Cochrane systematic review (Jacobson 2009) including 47 studies looked at the effects of patient reminders and/or recall systems in improving immunisation rates.

 

Studies included were randomised controlled trials, controlled before and after studies, and interrupted time series. All of them were in HIC and only in English.

 

The methodology quality of the studies was assessed using a previously published set of criteria.

 

Interventions:

  • letters to patients (22 studies)
  • postcards (9 studies)
  • telephone calls (8 studies)
  • computerised phone messages (5 studies)
  • post card and phone in combination (4 studies)
  • tracking and outreach (2 studies)
  • provider and patient reminders (6 studies)

 

Findings:

 

Jacobson 2009 reported the effects of the different interventions on rates of children receiving influenza vaccinations, pre-school children receiving routine immunisations and adults (receiving influenza and other vaccines). Here, only pre-school routine immunisation outcomes are reported (odds ratios with 95% confidence intervals, all comparisons against control):

  • postcard reminder: 1.70 (0.85 to 3.42)
  • letter reminder: 1.58 (1.26 to 1.99)
  • phone reminder: 4.25 (1.85 to 9.75)
  • autodilaer: 1.43 (1.30 to 1.57)
  • card and phone: 1.45 (1.11 to 1.89)
  • patient and provider reminder: 3.57 (1.03 to 12.41)
  • any patient reminder: 1.47 (1.28 to 1.68)
  • tracking and outreach: 1.37 (0.98 to 1.92)

 

Highlighted by the authors:

  • the observed effects were consistent across several settings (private practices, academic medical centers and public health department clinics), for both adults and children.

Considerations:

  • the interventions assessed required a certain level of technology and all studies took place in HIC, therefore, generalising on other settings is not possible.
  • The fact that only English or English translated studies were included could have introduced a bias in the reported findings.
